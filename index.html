Environmental Impact

This chart shows what “areas” the different materials have impact on. The scale ranges from 1-6. A score of 6 means relatively high impact and a score of 1 is “relatively low impact”. A score of 0 means “not applicable”.

This is meant to give a visual overview of what the problem areas of different materials are, it does not suggest that one is better than the other. This data is based on research by DEFRA. The study can be found in the bottom of this page.

*GHG stands for greenhouse gas.

Cotton

  • Pros

  • Natural fibre

  • Biodegradable

  • Recyclable (with limitations)
  • Cons

  • Uses large amounts of water in production. Approx 20,000 litres per kilo

  • A pesticide intensive crop, harmful to humans

  • Uses agricultural land, needed by people to grow their own food

Wool

  • Pros

  • Biodegradable

  • Generally a durable material

  • Does not need a lot of washing

  • Generally environmentally friendly dying process
  • Cons

  • Animal abuse issues within the wool farming industries

  • Greenhouse gas emissions from keeping livestock

Nylon

  • Pros

  • Recyclable (with limitations)

  • Generally a durable material
  • Cons

  • Does not biodegrade

  • Energy and chemical heavy production process

  • Produced by oil-based and fossil fuel substances

  • Plastic micro-fibers can be released during washing and end up in our water supplies

  • Emits a lot of greenhouse gases in production

Polyester

  • Pros

  • A generally durable material

  • Recyclable (with limitations)
  • Cons

  • Does not biodegrade

  • Energy and chemical heavy production process

  • Made from fossil fuels and oil-based substances

  • Plastic micro-fibers release when washing and end up in our water supplies

  • Emits a lot of greenhouse gases in production

Acrylic

  • Pros

  • Generally a durable material
  • Cons

  • Not easily recyclable

  • Doesn’t biodegrade

  • Energy and chemical heavy production process

  • Made from fossil fuels and oil-based substances

  • Plastic micro-fibers release when washing and end up in our water supplies

  • Emits a lot of greenhouse gases in production

Viscose

  • Pros

  • Made from wood pulp, renewable source
  • Cons

  • Not easily recyclable

  • Generally needs to be dry-cleaned

  • Generally non-durable

  • Production causes deforestation

  • Production causes air-pollution

  • Production pollutes water

  • Production uses chemicals, toxic to humans and the environment

What can I do?

Try to buy less, think about if you really need it.
Buy things that will last, and that will keep its value after use.
Find a second hand option.
Choose items that are biodegradable and/or easily recyclable.
Avoid unnecessary mixtures of clothing as these are more difficult to recycle.
Although, mixed fabrics can sometimes be more durable. Make an informed decision.
Look for organic and recycled materials. Although, there are certain issues with these types of materials as well.
No consumption is the best consumption.
